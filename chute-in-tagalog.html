13. During World War 2, a military parachutist fell 0.37km from an airplane without being able to open his chute, but luckily was able to land in snow, suffering only with minor injuries. Assume that his speed at impact was 56 m/s, and his mass, including his gear was 85kg, and that the magnitude of force on him from the snow was at the survivable limit of 1.2 x 10^5 N. What is the magnitude of the impulse on him from the snow?


Answer:

The magnitude of the impulse of the parachutist on the snow is 3,025,200 kg·m/s

Explanation:

Here, we are going to use the equation of Impulse which is also equivalent to the change in momentum.

Impulse:

I = F· Δt

where

F     is the force

Δt   is the change in time

Change in momentum:

Δp = m(vf - vi)

where

Δp     is the momentum

m       is the mass

vi, vf       is the initial and final velocity respectively

Now, the impulse is equal to the change in momentum (I = Δp), therefore,

F· Δt = m(vf - vi)

Here, we can solve first for Δt.

Solving the problem

Let us use the given equation

F· Δt = m(vf - vi)

Then, substitute the given data

1.2 x 10⁵ N · Δt = (85 kg)(56 m/s)

Divide both sides by (85 kg)(56 m/s), therefore

Δt = 25.21 seconds

Now, we can solve for the impulse by using the formula

I = F· Δt

I = (1.2 x 10⁵ N)(25.21 s)

I = 3,025,200 kg·m/s

Therefore, the impulse of the parachutist on the snow is 3,025,200 kg·m/s

14. When a drag strip vehicle reaches a velocity of 60 m/s, it begins a negative acceleration by releasing a drag chute and applying its brakes. While reducing its velocity back to zero, its acceleration along a straight line path is a constant −6.0 m/s2. What displacement does it undergo during this deceleration period?


Answer:

The displacement is 300 m .

Explanation:

Formula for Motion in a Straight Line

a. [tex]V_{1} =V_{0} +at[/tex]

b. [tex]x=V_{0} t+\frac{1}{2} at^2[/tex]

where

[tex]V_{1}[/tex]     is the final velocity, unit is in m/s

[tex]V_{0}[/tex]     is the initial velocity, unit is in m/s

[tex]a[/tex]      is the acceleration, unit is in m/[tex]s^2[/tex]

[tex]t[/tex]       is the time duration, unit is in seconds (s)

[tex]x[/tex]       is the distance, unit is in meters (m)

Given information

[tex]V_{0}[/tex] = 60 m/s

[tex]V_{1}[/tex] = 0             because the problem stated that when the vehicle apply

                      brakes, its velocity reduces back to zero

a = - 6.0 m/[tex]s^2[/tex]

Solving the problem

1. Use the first formula to solve for the time

[tex]V_{1} =V_{0} +at[/tex]

0 = 60 m/s + (- 6 m/[tex]s^2[/tex]) t

6t = 60

Divide both sides of the equation by 6

t = [tex]\frac{60}{6}[/tex]

t = 10 seconds

2. Use the second formula to solve for the distance

[tex]x=V_{0} t+\frac{1}{2} at^2[/tex]

Substitute t = 10s to the formula, we have:

[tex]x=(60m/s)(10s)+\frac{1}{2} (-6m/s^2)(10)^2[/tex]

x = 600 m - 300 m

x = 300 m

Therefore, the displacement of the drag strip vehicle is 300 m

19. A 12.0 − package in a mail-sorting room slides 2.00 down a chute that is inclined at 53.0° below the horizontal. The coefficient of kinetic friction between the package and the chute’s surface is 0.40. What is the net work done on the package?


Answer:

[tex]\therefore[/tex] The net force work done on the package is 131.22J

Explanation:

To solve for net work done on the package, first is to solve for the work done by the friction then solve for work done by the gravity. Afterwards, solve for the work done by the normal force. Because the net work done on the package is sum of the friction, gravity and normal force.

Work Done by the friction:

[tex]\boxed{\bold{W_{friction} = -M_kmg \ cos \theta \cdot slide}} \\\\ \rm{= -0.40 \cdot 12.0 \cdot 9.8 \cdot \cos 53.0 \cdot 2.00} \\\\ \rm{=-94.08\cos 53} \\\\ \therefore \rm{W_{friction} \approx-56.62}[/tex]

Worek Done by the gravity:

[tex]\boxed{\bold{W_{gravity} = mg \ sin\theta \cdot slide}} \\\\ \rm{= 12.0 \cdot 9.8 \cdot \sin 53.0 \cdot 2.00} \\\\ \rm{=235.2\sin\ 53.0} \\\\ \therefore \rm{W_{gravity}\approx 187.84}[/tex]

Work done by the normal force is equivalent to 0, because as [tex]\theta[/tex] is 90 that will be [tex]\cos 90[/tex] and will be resulting 0.

Now, solving for the net work done on the package.

[tex]\boxed{\bold{W_{Net \ Force} = W_{friction} + W_{gravity} + W_{Normal \ Force}}} \\\\ \rm{= -56.62 + 187.84 + 0} \\\\ \rm{W_{Net\ Force} = 131.22J}[/tex]

[tex]\therefore[/tex] The net force work done on the package is 131.22J
