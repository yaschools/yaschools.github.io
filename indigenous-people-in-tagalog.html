8. difference of tribal people and indigenous people


Answer:

Indigenous refers to being a member of the original inhabitants of a particular place, whereas tribal refers to being a member of a tribe, a distinct people that share a common ancestor, culture, beliefs, customs, and traditions.

Explanation:

Indigenous peoples refer to descents from those who inhabited geographical regions at the time of colonization, conquest, or establishment of present state boundaries. Tribal people, on the other hand, are people whose cultural, social, and economic conditions differ from other sections of the national community.

20. Can indigenous livelihood and technology sustain the needs of indigenous people


Answer:

For indigenous peoples, “poverty and prosperity” is intertwined with the status of their ownership, control and access to lands, territories and resources, along with the practice of their traditional occupations, sustainable resource management systems and self-governance. Their land is also the material base of their distinct identity, spirituality and culture, which are essential for their dignity and wellbeing. Thus, indigenous peoples’ perspective on being “poor” is being landless, and “prosperity” means having the security to manage and utilize their land and resources to meet their needs. The provision of basic social services such as appropriate health, education and infrastructures, and livelihood support are needed when addressing poverty in the changing condition of indigenous peoples

28. In what way the rights of Indigenous people differ from the right of non-Indigenous people ? Is there stated in Indigenous people that doesn't exist to non-indigenous people Yes, cite some examples and explain briefly your answer.​


Answer:

Indigenous peoples, also known as First Nations, Aboriginal peoples, or Tribal peoples, are groups of people who are the original inhabitants of a specific region or country, and who have a distinct culture, language, and identity. Indigenous peoples often have rights that are recognized and protected by national and international laws, and these rights may differ from those of non-Indigenous peoples.

One example of a right that is specifically recognized for Indigenous peoples is the right to self-determination. This means that Indigenous peoples have the right to determine their own political status and pursue their own economic, social, and cultural development. This may include the right to control and manage their own lands, resources, and institutions.

Other examples of rights that may be recognized for Indigenous peoples include:

The right to maintain and revitalize their cultural practices and traditions.The right to education in their own language and in a manner that reflects their culture and traditions.The right to participate in decisions that affect their lands, resources, and rights.The right to receive fair compensation for the use of their lands or resources.These rights may not be recognized or protected for non-Indigenous peoples in the same way. It is important to note that the rights of Indigenous peoples can vary depending on the specific laws and policies of a given country or region.
