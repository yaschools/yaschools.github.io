Capital formation is a term used to describe the net capital accumulation during an accounting period for a particular country. The term refers to additions of capital goods, such as equipment, tools, transportation assets, and electricity. Countries need capital goods to replace the older ones that are used to produce goods and services. If a country cannot replace capital goods as they reach the end of their useful lives, production declines. Generally, the higher the capital formation of an economy, the faster an economy can grow its aggregate income.

9. Ano ang ibig-sabihin ng Capital Goods? Circulating Capital? Fixed Capital? Free Capital? Specialized Capital? Financial Capital? Depreciation Capital? Investment Capiral? Saving Capital? Loan Capital?


Fixed capital is a concept in economics and accounting, first theoretically analyzed in some depth by the economist David Ricardo. It refers to any kind of real or physical capital (fixed asset) that is not used up in the production of a product. It contrasts with circulating capital such as raw materials, operating expenses and the like.

So fixed capital is that portion of the total capital outlay that is invested in fixed assets (such as land, buildings, vehicles, plant and equipment), that stay in the business almost permanently—or at the very least, for more than one accounting period. Fixed assets can be purchased by a business, in which case the business owns them. They can also be leased, hired or rented, if that is cheaper or more convenient, or if owning the fixed asset is practically impossible (for legal or technical reasons).

Refining the classical distinction between fixed and circulating capital in Das Kapital, Karl Marx emphasizes that the distinction is really purely relative, i.e. it refers only to the comparative rotation speeds (turnover time) of different types of physical capital assets. Fixed capital also "circulates", except that the circulation time is much longer, because a fixed asset may be held for 5, 10 or 20 years before it has yielded its value and is discarded for its salvage value. A fixed asset may also be resold and re-used, which often happens with vehicles and planes.

In national accounts, fixed capital is conventionally defined as the stock of tangible, durable fixed assets owned or used by resident enterprises for more than one year. This includes plant, machinery, vehicles and equipment, installations and physical infrastructures, the value of land improvements, and buildings.

The European system of national and regional accounts (ESA95) explicitly includes produced intangible assets (e.g. mineral exploitation, computer software, copyright protected entertainment, literary and artistics originals) within the definition of fixed assets.

Land itself is not included in the statistical concept of fixed capital, even though it is a fixed asset. The main reason is that land is not regarded as a product (a reproducible good). But the value of land improvements is included in the statistical concept of fixed capital, being regarded as the creation of value-added through production.

17. what industry that requires high capital investment​


Answer:

There are several industries that require high capital investment, including:

Energy: The energy industry, particularly the oil and gas, nuclear, and renewable energy sectors, requires significant capital investment to fund exploration, drilling, and production activities, as well as the construction of power plants, transmission lines, and other infrastructure.

Manufacturing: Many manufacturing industries, such as automotive, aerospace, and heavy equipment, require high capital investment to fund the development and production of new products, as well as the construction and maintenance of manufacturing facilities.

Infrastructure: Industries such as construction, transportation, and telecommunications require significant capital investment to fund the development and construction of roads, bridges, airports, rail lines, and other infrastructure.

Pharmaceuticals: The pharmaceutical industry requires high capital investment for research and development, clinical trials and FDA approvals.

Real Estate: real estate industry requires high capital investment for the development of commercial and residential properties.

Technology: The technology industry, such as semiconductors, software, and hardware, requires high capital investment for research and development, as well as the production and marketing of new products and services.

25. A fixed capital investment of Php. 10,000,000 is required for the proposed manufacturing plant and an estimated working capital of Php. 2,000,000. Annual depreciation is estimated to be 10% of the fixed capital investment. Determine the rate of return on the total investment if the annual profit is Php. 2,500,000


The rate of return on the total investment is 12.5%

Let us list first the numbers given to us:

Fixed Capital = PHP 10, 000, 000

Working Capital = PHP 2, 000, 000

Annual Depreciation = 10% of PHP 10, 000, 000

Annual Profit = PHP 2, 500, 000

Then, let’s calculate the Annual Depreciation to turn the unit into PHP. To do that we must multiply the Fixed Capital to .10

10, 000, 000 x .10 = 1, 000, 000

Total Capital Cost: PHP 12, 000, 000

Annual Depreciation: PHP 1, 000, 000

Annual Profit: PHP 2, 500, 000

Annual Profit - Annual Depreciation = Actual Profit

2, 500, 000 - 1, 000, 000 = 1, 500, 000

Actual Profit = PHP 1, 500, 000

To calculate the rate of investment, we must use the formula:

Actual Profit/Capital Total = Rate of Investment

1, 500, 000/12, 000, 000 = 0.125

We turn this decimal into a percentage by multiplying the number by 100.

The rate of investment is 12.5%.
