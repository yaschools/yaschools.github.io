Answered August 23, 2017

If you could have your own business one day, what would it be?

What type of business would you want to start?

[A2A] Good questions are the path to good answers. I’d like more detail in your question (and in most questions I see). Why are you asking? What do you hope to get out of my answer?

If you’re looking for someone to tell you, “This is the exact sequence of events you need to accomplish for you to be successful,” no one can give you that.

Even if they give you the road map from their own experience, you still have to figure things out on your own. The map is not the territory.

Consider that we all have 24 hours in a day, and that what we feel seems to be “equal” no matter what the scale is. A homebody who does little all day can feel the same “amount” of stress as a CEO of a 1000-person company…in spite of the fact that the agonizing decision for the homebody would be a six-second, over-and-forgotten choice for the CEO.

So you’re going to feel stress no matter what business you get into. Also, I can tell you the amount of work to make a high ticket sale is the same…or less(!)…than that needed for a low ticket sale.

So the question really is: What game do you want to play?

No path is easier or better than another.

It comes down to your interests, skills, desires, beliefs. What do you believe you can do? What do you believe is beyond your level? (These are changeable goalposts, by the way; I talk about them in my money tolerance videos).

We tend to get what we believe we’ll get.

You think $10,000 is “a lot” of money? OK. You’ll have trouble selling luxury yachts. If this is true for you right now, pick something at the top end of your belief of what is possible for you to sell and earn…something at the bottom of your “a lot of money” range. That’ll excite you while leaving the possibility of success open. If I thought $10K was “a lot” of money, I’d pick a business with price ranges of things to sell around $5K - $8K.

But that’s me. I’m not a “low ticket, high volume” guy. You won’t see me pushing candied apples, or supplements, or grown-up coloring books. All of which you can make money at. That’s my preference. I’m interested in the individual transaction value. The shock of a single transaction “bump” on my bank account—that’s what gets me excited.

You? I don’t know. What do you think?

There’s a sneaking suspicion I have that I could really blow it up with a repeatable purchase business like toilet paper, alcohol, or makeup. I love the idea. Maybe I’ll get to it one day—I understand all the moving parts. The individual transaction value just doesn’t turn my crank, though. It’s a secondary idea, one I might fund down the road out of profits from my preferred style of making money.

I ran a consultancy for years like this: one-time, high ticket “bumps” to income. It was tough to scale and I didn’t really try to scale it. Early this year I started a business that could scale with a partner. Surprise, it includes high ticket selling with a nice “bump” on every revenue transaction. That’s the game I want to play. That’s the prize I’m willing to endure the stress for. You? Maybe you’ll be happy making $30 apeice on Amazon dropshipping…and maybe you’ll make more money in total revenue than I do. There’s no right or wrong…except in relation to your own preferences.

We will only take action on what we believe we can do.

We will only do what we believe is possible.

What we believe is possible is a moveable range. Remember that, always. I see more revenue in a week than I did in half a year as a salaried employee.

No one can tell you what to do.

You have to sit down and do an honest appraisal of yourself. What are your likes and dislikes? What’s your current money tolerance? What do you believe you can do, and can’t do? What excites you, what bores you?

Then build your business idea around those things.

And remember, each of those factors is a changeable thing, completely under your control. You can change your likes. You can raise your money tolerance. You can expand your sphere of influence and control. You can get excited about anything you want.

6. Additional Activities LET'S TAKE A SHORTCUT Problem. It has been a routine for Sir Jason to go to the church every afternoon after work, Find the shortest distance from his work going to school​ with solution pls


Answer: The shortest distance from Sir Jayson's work to the church is 5m.

[tex]{}[/tex]

We can see that the shortest distance (d) from Sir Jayson's work to the church is a hypotenuse of a right-angled triangle with base 4m and altitude 3m.

Find d using Pythagorean theorem:

d² = 3² + 4²

d = [tex]\sf{ \sqrt{9 + 16}}[/tex]

d = [tex]\sf{ \sqrt{25}}[/tex]

d = 5
