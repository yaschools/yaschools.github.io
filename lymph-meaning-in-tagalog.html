13. How are blood, interstitial fluid and lymph related?​


Answer:

As the interstitial fluid begins to accumulate, it is picked up and removed by tiny lymphatic vessels and returned to the blood. As soon as the interstitial fluid enters the lymph capillaries, it is called lymph. Returning the fluid to the blood prevents edema and helps to maintain normal blood volume and pressure.

15. Explain the immune response in a lymph node.


The immune response in a lymph node involves a complex series of processes that are triggered when the body is exposed to a foreign substance, such as a virus or bacteria.

When a lymph node is stimulated, it becomes enlarged and activated. This process begins when immune cells called T-cells and B-cells, which are found in the lymph node, recognize and bind to foreign substances, such as antigens. This binding triggers the activation and proliferation of these immune cells, which begins the immune response.

The activated immune cells then produce and release a variety of substances, such as cytokines and antibodies, which help to coordinate the immune response and fight off the foreign substance.

As the immune response progresses, the lymph node may also attract other immune cells, such as macrophages and neutrophils, which help to destroy the foreign substance and remove it from the body.

Overall, the immune response in a lymph node involves the activation and proliferation of immune cells, the production of immune substances, and the coordination of other immune cells to fight off a foreign substance and protect the body from harm.
