13. Which part of the ear amplifies soundA. Outer earB. Inner earC. Middle ear​


Answer:

A. Outer ear

Explanation:

The auricle (pinna) is the visible portion of the outer ear. It collects sound waves and channels them into the ear canal (external auditory meatus), where the sound is amplified. The sound waves then travel toward a flexible, oval membrane at the end of the ear canal called the eardrum, or tympanic membrane.

The auricle (the outer part of your ear) and the ear canal (the passage down to the eardrum) serve not only to collect and funnel sound, they also serve to amplify sound. The ear canal, specifically, amplifies sound in the high frequencies (for an adult, typically in the region between 2000-4000 Hz).

20. Function of every part of ear -pinna -outer ear -middle ear -inner ear -temporal bone -incus -malleus -ear drum -ear canal -semicircular canals -vestibular nerves -auditory nerve -eustachian tube -cochlea


EAR PARTS AND IT'S FUNCTIONS

Pinna: The pinna, also known as the auricle, is the outermost part of the ear that helps to collect and funnel sound waves into the ear canal.

Outer ear: The outer ear consists of the pinna and the ear canal. The ear canal is a tube-like structure that carries sound waves to the eardrum.

Middle ear: The middle ear is located behind the eardrum and contains the ossicles, which are the three smallest bones in the human body: the malleus, incus, and stapes. These bones amplify sound waves and transmit them from the eardrum to the inner ear.

Inner ear: The inner ear contains the cochlea, which is responsible for converting sound waves into electrical signals that are sent to the brain via the auditory nerve. The inner ear also contains the vestibular system, which helps to maintain balance and spatial orientation.

Temporal bone: The temporal bone is a bone located on the side of the skull that houses the structures of the inner ear.

Incus and Malleus: The incus and malleus are two of the three ossicles located in the middle ear. They work together to amplify and transmit sound waves from the eardrum to the inner ear.

Ear drum: The eardrum, or tympanic membrane, is a thin, cone-shaped membrane located at the end of the ear canal. It vibrates in response to sound waves, which causes the ossicles to move and transmit the sound to the inner ear.

Ear canal: The ear canal is a tube-like structure that carries sound waves from the pinna to the eardrum.

Semicircular canals: The semicircular canals are part of the vestibular system in the inner ear and are responsible for detecting changes in head position and movement.

Vestibular nerves: The vestibular nerves are a pair of nerves that transmit information from the semicircular canals and other vestibular organs to the brain.

Auditory nerve: The auditory nerve is a nerve that transmits electrical signals from the cochlea to the brain, where they are processed and interpreted as sound.

Eustachian tube: The Eustachian tube is a narrow tube that connects the middle ear to the back of the throat. It helps to equalize air pressure between the middle ear and the outside environment.

Cochlea: The cochlea is a spiral-shaped organ in the inner ear that is responsible for converting sound waves into electrical signals that are sent to the brain via the auditory nerve.
