1. what should be done so that you are not offloaded at immigration ?​


Answer:

what should be done so that you are not offloaded at immigration ?

1. Make sure you have valid travel documents, such as a valid passport, visa, and any other required documents.

2. Ensure that your passport is up to date and that it will not expire during the duration of your trip.

3. Double-check that the information on your travel documents (such as name, date of birth, etc.) is correct and matches the information on your airline ticket.

4. If you are traveling to a country where you need a visa, make sure that you have obtained the required visa in advance.

5. Be prepared to answer questions honestly and accurately during the immigration process.

6. Be polite and courteous when interacting with immigration officials.

7. Follow all instructions given by immigration officials.

18. 5 policy government in dubai​


Answer:

Legislature

The Federal National Council (al-Majlis al-Watani al-Ittihadi) is the UAE's legislative body and consists of 40 members. The body only has advisory powers.[3] Twenty of the members are indirectly elected by the hand-picked 12% of Emirati citizens who have voting rights through an electoral college, while the other twenty are appointed by the rulers of each emirate.[15][16][17] According to Reuters, "the process of selecting the people who can either elect or be elected is opaque."[15]

Political parties are banned.

The FNC is the main consultative body in the UAE and has both a legislative and supervisory role accorded by the Constitution.

Since the council's inception, the following have been selected as speakers:[18] Thani Abdullah Humaid, Taryam Omran Taryam, Hilal bin Ahmed bin Lootah, Al Haj bin Abdullah Al Muhairbi, Mohammed Khalifa Habtour, Saeed Mohammad Al Gandi, Abdul Aziz Al Ghurair, Mohammad Al-Murr, and Amal Al Qubaisi since 2015.

Federal Judiciary

Main article: Judicial system in the United Arab Emirates

The Federal Judiciary is a constitutionally completely independent body (under Article 94) and includes the Federal Supreme Court and Courts of First Instance. Supreme Council of Rulers appoints the five judges headed by a president to the Supreme Court. The judges are responsible for deciding if federal laws are constitutional, mediating between inter-emirate disputes.[14] It also possesses the authority to try cases involving cabinet and senior federal officials. Although secular law is applied, the basis of legislation is Sharia (Islamic Law) and involves three of the four schools including (mainly) Maliki, but also the Hanbali and Shafi'i schools.[10]

Criticism

On 2 April 2021, 91-year-old German philosopher Juergen Habermas rejected the Sheikh Zayed Book Award worth 750,000 UAE dirhams prize money. Habermas earlier accepted the award, but later called it “a wrong decision”, which he corrected by rejecting it in April 2021. In a critical statement, Juergen cited his previous unawareness of the fact that the awarding institution had close connections with the existing political system of the country, which is a dictatorship as cited in a 2020 report published by Amnesty International.[19] [20]

Political reform and Arab spring

In early 2007, the United Arab Emirates launched the 'UAE Government Strategy' for the years ahead, which covered twenty-one topics in six different sectors including social development, economic development, public sector development, justice and safety, infrastructure and rural areas development. The initiative is meant to reevaluate and advance these sectors towards top global standards by facilitating better continuous cooperation between federal and local governments with increased efficiency, training, Emiratisaion, ministry empowerment, upgrading of services, improving civil service and legislation review.

Subsequently, Abu Dhabi announced implementation of its own policy to modernize public administration practices and government performance in 2007–2008. Plans for reevaluation were laid out in areas including economy, energy, tourism, health, education, labour, civil services, culture and heritage, good control, urban planning, transport, environment, health and safety, municipal affairs, police and emergency services, electronic government, women and legislative reform. Abu Dhabi hopes advancements towards global standards in these areas will improve the quality of services for its residents as well as attract future investment towards further modernizing the Emirate.[14]

The country did not see the type of trouble other Arab countries saw during the Arab spring. There were minor protests. Some people were arrested and some had their nationality revoked.[21]

The Economist Intelligence Unit rated the United Arab Emirates an "authoritarian regime" in 2019.[
