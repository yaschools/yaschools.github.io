What is PHOTOGRAPHY?

Photography was initially regarded as a strictly technical process, that of capturing visible images through light motion on light sensitive surfaces, in its early stages during the late nineteenth century. In reality, its very name – derived from the Greek words "images" (light) and "graphos" (writing) – expresses this process literally. Photography was not immediately considered art in contrast to the well known disciplines of painting and sculpture. But it wasn't long before the artistry of twentieth-century photographers lifted this "light writing" to its own aesthetic form. Photography is the technology, art, and tradition of making long-lasting photographs by capturing light or other electromagnetic radiation, either electronically through an image sensor or chemically with a light-sensitive medium like photographic film.

Mechanics

The fundamental components of a modern digital SLR still camera

A sensor captures light photons, which are mostly from the visible spectrum for human vision but can also be from other parts of the electromagnetic spectrum. Light enters an enclosed box through a converging or convex lens, and an image is captured on a light-sensitive medium in both cameras (mainly a transition metal-halide). The amount of time that light will reach the camera is regulated by a shutter mechanism. Most cameras also have a viewfinder, which displays the scene to be filmed, as well as the ability to adjust focus and exposure so that it is not too light or too dark.

Shutter

The shutter, along with the aperture, is one of two methods for controlling the volume of light that enters the camera. The shutter controls how long the light-sensitive surface is open to light. Light enters the camera through the aperture, exposing the film or sensor to light, and then the shutter shuts.

14. How does shutter speed affect a photograph?​


Answer:

The longer the shutter speed, the more light strikes the sensor, resulting in a brighter image. And the faster the shutter speed, the less light reaches the sensor, resulting in a darker image. Besides brightness, shutter speed also controls how motion is captured in your photo.

What is the relationship between aperture and shutter speed How can you describe this relationship?

Aperture, shutter speed and ISO combine to control how bright or dark the image is (the exposure). Using different combinations of aperture, shutter speed and ISO can achieve the same exposure. A larger aperture allows more light to hit the sensor and therefore the shutter speed can be made faster to compensate.

There is a reciprocal relationship between shutter speed and aperture. You can get the same amount of light if you change the shutter speed and aperture settings at equivalent amounts. For example, 1/30 at F5. 6 is the same as 1/8 at F11.
