2. What is the bystander effect? . . . . .


Answer:

Bystander effect, the inhibiting influence of the presence of others on a person's willingness to help someone in need.Research has shown that, even in an emergency, a bystander is less likely to extend help when he or she is in the real or imagined presence of others than when he or she is alone.

4. Someone met a vehicular accident. You were a bystander and decided to help. You see that blood is gushing from a cut on the palm of the victim. One bystander suggested using a belt to tie of the blood supply from the wrist to the affected hand. What will you do?


Answer:

If I were a bystander who came across a victim of a vehicular accident with a cut on the palm of their hand and blood is gushing out, I would do the following:

Call for Emergency Medical Services: The first thing to do is to call for emergency medical services (911 or the local emergency number) to get professional medical help as soon as possible.

Apply Pressure: While waiting for medical help to arrive, I would apply direct pressure to the wound using a clean cloth or bandage to try to stop the bleeding.

Elevate the Affected Hand: I would elevate the affected hand above the level of the heart, if possible, to reduce blood flow to the affected area and minimize bleeding.

Do Not Use a Belt: I would not use a belt or any other tourniquet to cut off the blood supply to the affected hand. Applying a tourniquet can be dangerous as it can cause the tissues to die from lack of oxygen and can lead to permanent damage or even amputation of the affected limb.

Monitor the Victim: While waiting for medical help, I would closely monitor the victim for any changes in their condition, including their breathing and consciousness.

In any medical emergency situation, it is important to stay calm and act quickly while keeping the victim's safety as the top priority.
