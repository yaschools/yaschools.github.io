9. short story that represents filipino culture, environment,history​


Once upon a time in the Philippines, there was a small village nestled in the heart of a lush forest. The villagers lived a simple life, relying on the bounty of the forest and the sea for their livelihood. They were a community of hardworking and hospitable people, always ready to lend a hand to their neighbors.

One day, a group of foreign invaders landed on the shores of the village. They came with weapons and a thirst for gold and power. The villagers were caught off guard and had no means to defend themselves against the invaders. They watched in horror as their homes were burned, their crops destroyed, and their loved ones taken captive.

But there was one brave young man named Jose who refused to give up. He remembered the stories his grandfather had told him about the bravery of their ancestors who fought against foreign invaders in the past. Jose knew that he had to act fast if he wanted to save his village.

He rallied the villagers and convinced them to use their knowledge of the forest to their advantage. They set traps, made weapons out of bamboo, and used the element of surprise to fight back against the invaders. The villagers fought with all their might, and in the end, they emerged victorious.

From that day on, the villagers lived in peace and prosperity. They celebrated their victory with a feast and a dance, and Jose became a hero in their eyes. He had shown them that even in the face of adversity, they could overcome any obstacle if they worked together and never gave up.

This story represents the resilience and resourcefulness of the Filipino people, who have faced countless challenges throughout their history but have always found a way to persevere. It also highlights the importance of preserving the environment and the knowledge of our ancestors, which can be invaluable in times of crisis.

19. HOW DOES THE RAPID CHANGES IN THE ENVIRONMENT AFFECTS THE FILIPINO FAMILIES​


Answer:

When species interact with eaResearch into coevolution has thus far been focused on pairs of species that feature high levels of interaction and therefore strong interdependencies, for example the oak and the jay, or ants and the acacia trees they protect. An international team of researchers with the involvement of the University of Zurich has now shown that coevolutionary processes also occur in complex communities of more than two species. The researchers found that the coevolution of a species pair is not only driven by the direct relationships between the two species. Rather, coevolution is also affected indirectly by other species with which one or even both of the species in a pair do not interact directly. "Depending on the type of biological community, these indirect effects have an even greater impact on coevolution than direct interactions," says Jordi Bascompte, professor at the Department of Evolutionary Biology and Environmental Studies at the University of Zurich.
