China claims the largest portion of the South China Sea, using the so-called "nine-dash line" to delineate its territory. This claim is contested by several Southeast Asian countries, which have overlapping claims based on their own historical and legal arguments. The Philippines, in particular, has been vocal in challenging China's claims, leading to a high-profile legal case at the Permanent Court of Arbitration in The Hague in 2016.

ASEAN (Association of Southeast Asian Nations) has played an important role in addressing the South China Sea dispute. ASEAN has also engaged with China through the ASEAN-China Dialogue, which was established in 1997. However, some observers argue that ASEAN's effectiveness in dealing with the South China Sea dispute has been limited. One challenge is that ASEAN operates on a consensus-based decision-making process, which can be slow and sometimes results in watered-down statements that fail to address the root causes of the conflict. China has also been accused of using its economic and political influence to divide ASEAN and prevent it from taking a unified stance on the issue.

Despite these challenges, there have been instances where ASEAN has made efforts to bridge diplomacy in the South China Sea dispute. For example, in 2017, ASEAN and China agreed on a framework for a code of conduct in the South China Sea, which aimed to promote confidence-building measures and prevent the escalation of tensions. However, the framework has yet to be translated into a binding code of conduct, and the dispute remains unresolved.
