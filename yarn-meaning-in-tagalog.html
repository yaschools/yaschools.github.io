Past-1. Blue Jeans and Denim Fabric

Levi Strauss and Jacob Davis in 1873 invented blue jeans in response to the need for laborers for durable men's workwear. The traditional fabric used in blue jeans is denim, a durable cotton twill textile. Historically, denim was made of silk and wool in Nimes, France (hence the name "de Nim"), and not of the all-cotton variety we are familiar with today.

2. FoxFibre®

In the 1980s, Sally Fox's passion for natural fibers led her to reinvent the naturally colored cotton used in cotton fabrics, mostly as a response to the pollution caused via the bleaching and dying processes performed in coloring cotton fabrics. Fox crossbred brown cotton, which also produced green cotton, with the aim to develop longer fibers and richer colors.

In turn, Fox's organic discoveries help to preserve the environment and can be found in everything from underwear to bed sheets.

3. GORE-TEX®

GORE-TEX® is a registered trademark and the best-known product of W. L. Gore & Associates, Inc. The trademarked product was introduced in 1989. The fabric, based on a Gore-held patent for a membrane technology, is specifically engineered to be breathable water and wind-proof material. The phrase "Guaranteed to Keep You Dry®" is also a Gore-owned registered trademark, part of the GORE-TEX® warranty.

Wilbert L. and Genevieve Gore founded the company on January 1, 1958, in Newark, Delaware. The Gores set out to explore opportunities for fluorocarbon polymers, especially polytetrafluoroethylene. The current CEO is their son Bob. Wilbert Gore was posthumously inducted into The Plastics Hall of Fame in 1990.

Present-4.Viscose Fabric

Viscose is a semi-synthetic rayon fabric made from wood pulp and is used as a substitute for silk. The drape and smoothness are similar in viscose which gives the fabric a luxury feel and makes it a prominent substitute for silk. The wood pulp is converted into fabric by treating it with chemicals. It is an absorbent and lightweight fabric with a breathable and soft feel to it, which makes the fabric highly preferred. The fabric by itself is not elastic but can be made so by the addition of spandex. Therefore, you can see that there are different types of fabrics used in the textile business.

5.Khadi Fabric

Khadi, also known as khaddar is a handwoven natural fabric made primarily with cotton. Other versions of khadi fabric also include silk and wool. The texture of khadi fabric is rugged in texture and feels comfortable to be worn in summer as well as winter. Today the khadi fabric is available in various variations, prints, and designs ranging from Kantha to block prints. Moreover, the fabric is available in a wide range of t-shirts, dresses, and pants. Due to industrialization, khadi making is now done through machines. This has not only reduced the time of production but has also made the fabric available in new cuts and designs. Khadi is one of the most common fabric names used in India

It is commonly done to add decorative materials such as pearls, beads, quills, etc. Today, embroidery is done on hats, blankets, dresses, shirts, denim, etc. to enhance their look. Embroidery adds value to any simply dyed fabrics and makes them look pleasant and fashionable. Embroidered fabric today is made with computers to increase the efficiency as well as precision of work.

30. tie the end of the yarn to the first strips so that the yarn will not move when weaving​


The weaving process

In most cases, woven cloth is substantially longer in one direction than the other. The warp refers to the lengthwise threads, while the weft refers to the threads that are combined with the warp and lie widthwise (synonyms include "filling," "woof," and "shot," or "Shute"). Each individual length of weft stretching from one edge of the cloth to the other is termed a pick, or shot, and each individual thread from the warp of infinite length is called an end. Consecutive picks are usually lengths of one strand of weft yarn that are folded back on themselves again.

Before a length of weft is introduced in the warp in any way of weaving cloth (save the primitive form of darning), the warp is divided into two sheets across a short length extending from the cloth already made. Shedding is the procedure, and the shed is the gap between the sheets. In the picking operation, a pick of weft is subsequently laid between the two warp sheets. A new shed is then produced, with some or all of the ends in each sheet shifting over to the place formerly held by the other sheet, in line with the desired weave structure. The weft is clasped between two layers of warp in this manner.
