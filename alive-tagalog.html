16. THE FIRST PERSON TO BE ALIVE


The first person to be alive is a subject of debate among scientists and historians. It is believed that the first anatomically modern humans (Homo sapiens) appeared around 300,000 years ago. However, the concept of a "first person" is a difficult one to define and there were likely many different groups of early humans living at different times and places. Ultimately, it is impossible to say exactly who the first person to be alive was.

It is impossible to determine the first person to be alive as there is no definitive record or evidence of the first human being. The earliest known human fossils, which belong to the species Homo habilis, date back to approximately 2.8 million years ago. However, the origin of the human species is believed to have occurred much earlier, possibly around 7 million years ago. The exact timeline and details of human evolution are still a subject of ongoing research and debate among scientists.

21. is virus considired alive?


Answer:

For about a century, the scientific community has repeatedly changed its definition of a virus. First seen as poisons, then as life-forms, then as biological chemicals. As of today, viruses are thought to be in a somewhat gray area between living and non-living; they cannot replicate on their own, but they can do so in living cells, and can also affect the behavior of their host profoundly. The categorization of viruses as non-living during most of its history lead to an unintended mistake: it led most researchers to ignore viruses in their studies.

These behaviors are what led many to think of viruses existing within the border of chemistry and life. More poetically, virologists Marc H. V. Van Regenmortel of the University of Strasbourg and Brian W. J. Mahy of the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention have recently said that with their dependence on host cells, viruses lead “a kind of borrowed life.”
