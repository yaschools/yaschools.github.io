3. Discuss the differences and similarities of the Filipino versus Tagalog language.​


Answer:

The Differences Between Tagalog and Filipino are Rooted in History

Tagalog had primarily been spoken in Manila and the surrounding provinces in the 1930s when the Commonwealth Constitution was originally drawn up. This constitution had stipulations in it that provided for an official national language, but it did not specifically name Tagalog as that language. When the constitution was drawn up, Tagalog was only spoken by about 25% of the population.

Tagalog was the lingua franca of people who lived in or near the government capital, and by the 1970s, more than half of the Philippine population was using Tagalog to communicate with one another. Then, during the Aquino presidency in the latter half of the 1980s, the national language was officially labeled Filipino.

The Western Influence on Language

The purpose of this was to embrace the widespread preference for a number of words derived from English and Spanish, as well as the incorporation of “Western” letters such as j, c, x, z, and f. These were not natural sounds to the native citizens of those islands before the arrival of foreigners from Spain and the Americas, but they quickly became a part of the Filipino alphabet.

Filipino has incorporated a number of modern words from English and Spanish. For example, the Spanish word “diccionario” has been incorporated into Filipino as “diksyunaryio.” However, a Tagalog purist might insist on the native word for dictionary, which is “talatinigan.”

11. differentiate magma versus lava versus volcanic ash ​


Answer:

Magma, lava, and volcanic ash are all related to volcanic activity, but they have distinct differences. Here's how they differ:

Magma - Magma is a molten rock that is located beneath the Earth's surface. It is formed by the melting of rock due to high temperature and pressure, and it can contain gases, minerals, and other materials. Magma can rise towards the surface and can cause volcanic eruptions when it reaches the Earth's surface.

Lava - Lava is molten rock that has erupted onto the Earth's surface. It can flow or explode out of a volcano during an eruption, and it can form a variety of landforms such as volcanoes, lava domes, and lava fields. Lava is typically more viscous and slower moving than magma due to the cooling and crystallization that occurs as it moves towards the surface.

Volcanic Ash - Volcanic ash is a mixture of small, solid particles and gases that are ejected into the atmosphere during a volcanic eruption. It is formed by the explosive fragmentation of lava and rock, and it can travel long distances from the eruption site. Volcanic ash can cause a range of hazards, including respiratory problems, damage to crops, and disruption to transportation and communication systems.

In summary, magma is molten rock beneath the Earth's surface, lava is molten rock that has erupted onto the Earth's surface, and volcanic ash is a mixture of solid particles and gases that are ejected into the atmosphere during a volcanic eruption.

14. Right versus privilegesMoral Right versus Legal Right ​


Rights can be classified in different ways, and two common types of rights are moral rights and legal rights. Additionally, rights can also be categorized as either a right or a privilege.

1. Moral Right versus Legal Right:

Moral rights refer to rights that individuals inherently possess as human beings. These rights are considered to be universal and apply to everyone regardless of their nationality, race, gender, or religion. Examples of moral rights include the right to life, liberty, and security, as well as the right to free speech, assembly, and religion.

On the other hand, legal rights are rights that are established by law. They are granted and protected by the government and can vary from country to country. Legal rights can include things such as the right to a fair trial, the right to vote, and the right to own property.

2. Right versus Privilege:

A right is something that a person is entitled to by virtue of being a human being or a member of a particular society. It cannot be taken away without just cause, and it is usually protected by law. Examples of rights include the right to life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness.

A privilege, on the other hand, is a special advantage or benefit that is not available to everyone. It is usually granted by someone in a position of authority, and it can be taken away if the person abuses it or does not meet certain requirements. Examples of privileges include the privilege to drive a car or the privilege to attend a private school.

In summary, moral and legal rights are two different types of rights, while rights and privileges are two different concepts altogether. Understanding the differences between them is important in order to navigate the complex world of rights and responsibilities.
