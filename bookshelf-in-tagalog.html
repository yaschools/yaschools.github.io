5. A 1.12 kg book falls off the top of a 2.03 m bookshelf. How much work is required to put the book back on top of the bookshelf, assuming it is lifted with a constant velocity​?


Answer:

22.28J

Explanation:

Work is a force times a distance:

W=F∗d

We know the distance that the book needs to travel, but we need to sovle for the lifting force required to move it.

There are two forces acting upon the book: the lifting force and gravity. Since the book is moving with a constant velocity, that means the net force will be zero. Mathemetically, that would look like this:

Fnet=FG+Flift=0

Flift=−FG

We can expand the right side of the equation using Newton's second law:

Flift=−(mg)

Use the given mass and value of gravity to solve for the lifting force.

Flift=−(1.12kg∗−9.8ms2)

Flift=10.976N

Now that we have the force and the distance, we can solve for the work to lift the book.

W=10.976N∗2.03m

W=22.28J

This problem can also be solved using energy. Work is equal to the change in potential energy:

W=ΔPE

While on the ground, the book has zero potential energy. Once back on the shelf, the energy is equal to mgh. The work is thus also equal to mgh.

W=mgh=(1.12kg)(−9.8ms2)(2.03m)

W=22.28J

25. There are 4 math and 3 english books on the bookshelf. mathbooks should be placed on the left side of the bookshelf and english books on the right side of the bookshelf. How many ways are there to arrange the books?


Answer:

There are 4 math books and 3 English books, and we want to arrange them such that the math books are on the left side of the bookshelf and the English books are on the right side.

First, we need to choose the order of the math books. There are 4 math books, so there are 4 choices for the first book, 3 choices for the second book, 2 choices for the third book, and 1 choice for the fourth book. This gives us a total of 4 x 3 x 2 x 1 = 24 ways to arrange the math books.

Next, we need to choose the order of the English books. There are 3 English books, so there are 3 choices for the first book, 2 choices for the second book, and 1 choice for the third book. This gives us a total of 3 x 2 x 1 = 6 ways to arrange the English books.

Finally, we need to combine the arrangements of the math books and the English books. Since the order of the math books is fixed and the order of the English books is fixed, there is only one way to combine them.

Therefore, the total number of ways to arrange the books is:

24 x 6 x 1 = 144

There are 144 ways to arrange the books.
