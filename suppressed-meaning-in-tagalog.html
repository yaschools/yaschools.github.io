I'm not totally comfortable answering this question because it seems imprecise to me. 'Democracy' is a word susceptible of several interpretations and I'd think its 'suppression' would mean dominance by some kind of minority.

For whatever it may be worth, my opinion is that all modern so-called 'democratic' countries suppress democracy. What that means is that the people do not govern themselves, as is implied by the word democracy, but are ruled by small groups of unelected individuals who control the political infrastructure.

In place of democracy, power-seeking individuals institute a form of pseudo-democracy where control of the government is vested in organizations called political parties. The parties claim to represent the people, or, more properly, certain subsets of the people, but, instead, they only provide a power-base for their leaders. The party leaders control the government by controlling the officials the people elect to represent their interests.

The parties control the elected officials by arrogating to themselves the right to choose the candidates for public office and providing the finances and infrastructure necessary for the candidates' election. Since the elected officials are indebted to the parties for their selection and election, the parties are able to control the government. One of the subtle beauties of the system (from the party leaders' perspective) is that it allows the political leaders to create the myth that the people govern themselves because they are allowed to choose between the offerings of the competing parties.

The parties lure the money they need for the power they crave by selling the nation's laws to vested interests - which leads to the quip that "We have the best laws money can buy." In this sense, it is fair to say that suppressing democracy means the rise of corrupt government.

Tom Gregory mentions a practical method of suppressing democracy and goes on to describe the meaning of suppressing democracy as making the public vote meaningless. 17. when punishment subjugation or suppression​


Answer:

Three experiments using a multiple response technique (Dunham, 1978) with Mongolian gerbils were designed to investigage die effect of different methods of producing response suppression on nonsuppressed (alternative) behaviour. In the first experiment white noise was used to punish eating, running or digging, respectively, in three groups of gerbils. Noise suppressed the target behaviour in each group, and the behaviour suppressed seemed to determine which alternative would increase in duration to take its place. In the second experiment eating was suppressed with white noise, shock, or by removing the food. Regardless of the method of suppression, the same alternative–digging–increased to replace eating. In the third experiment eating was again suppressed by shock, food removal, or satiation. Again in the shock and removal groups digging increased, but in the satiated group running increased. We argue that these results are consistent with a description of response suppression that claims that the increase in alternative behaviours is secondary to the suppression mechanism and that the behaviour that will increase to replace the suppressed behavior is determined by the underlying momentary motivational hierarchy at the time of response suppression.
