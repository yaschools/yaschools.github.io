What is skeletal muscle?

The majority of your body's muscles are found in your skeleton. They make up between 30 and 40 percent of your body's mass. Tendons, strong bands of connective tissue, connect the bones and skeletal muscles in every area of your body. Your hamstrings, shoulders, and abdominal muscles are all skeletal muscles.

Because skeletal muscles are voluntary, you have control over when and how they contract. In order for your somatic nervous system to function, your nerves must send messages. As you reach for a book on a shelf, you make use of your neck, arm, and shoulder skeletal muscles.

There are skeletal muscles all across your body. They are located between bones. Skeletal muscles are composed of flexible muscular fibers with diameters ranging from less than half an inch to slightly over three inches. These fibers typically span the whole length of the muscle. As a result of the muscles' capacity to move bones when their fibers contract, you can carry out a range of motions (tighten).

The skeleton's muscle fibers are red and white. Because of the way they appear striped or striated, they are sometimes referred to as striated muscles. Cardiovascular muscles are striated, whereas smooth muscles are not.

Generally speaking, your skeletal muscles account for about 35% of your body weight, but this varies from person to person. In comparison to women, men have roughly 36% higher skeletal muscle mass. Additionally, those who are tall or obese typically have more muscular mass. Age causes a loss in muscle mass in both men and women.
