What is diffusion?

When a chemical goes from one area of high concentration to another of lower concentration, it diffuses. Diffusion occurs when particles in liquids and gases randomly collide and disperse. Diffusion is an essential function for living organisms because it controls how molecules enter and exit cells. Diffusion is the movement of a substance's individual molecules across a semipermeable barrier from an area of higher concentration to an area of lower concentration.

Any movement from a higher concentration to a lower concentration is referred to as diffusion. Diffusion is fueled by a gradient in the chemical potential or Gibbs free energy. When particles disperse, they diffuse. They go from a location where they are densely concentrated to one where they are less heavily concentrated. Diffusion occurs when the particles are allowed to move freely. In contrast to gases and particles dissolved in fluids, solids do not undergo diffusion.

The functions of a cell—absorption of nutrients, production of energy, and elimination of waste—all depend on diffusion. Examples of the materials cells need and the waste products they produce are given in the table.

Diffusion is one type of passive transport. For passive transportation, no energy is required. A single substance moves from a high concentration area to a low concentration area until the concentration is uniform throughout the area.
