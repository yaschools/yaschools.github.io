13. 2. what are the different superstitious beliefs of the Tagalog? How does it shapes the lives of the natives during that time? (10 points)​


Answer:

There are many different superstitious beliefs that were held by the Tagalog people, a culturally and linguistically diverse group who lived in what is now the central and southern regions of the Philippines. Some examples of these beliefs include:

Belief in good and bad omens: The Tagalog believed that certain events or objects could be signs of good or bad luck. For example, seeing a black cat might be considered a bad omen, while finding a four-leaf clover was considered a good one.

Belief in spirits and supernatural beings: The Tagalog believed in the existence of a variety of supernatural beings, such as spirits of the dead, guardian angels, and nature spirits. These beings were thought to be able to influence the lives of the living in both positive and negative ways.

Belief in magic and spells: The Tagalog believed in the power of magic and spells, and many people consulted with shamans or other spiritual practitioners in order to seek guidance or protection.

Belief in astrology: The Tagalog believed that the movements of the stars and planets could have an influence on people's lives, and many people consulted with astrologers to seek guidance on important decisions.

These superstitious beliefs shaped the lives of the Tagalog in many ways. For example, they may have influenced people's decision-making, caused them to take certain precautions or rituals to avoid bad luck, or led them to seek out the help of spiritual practitioners in order to address problems or seek blessings.

17. What are the belief systems present among the Tagalogs during the pre colonial period​


Answer:

HERE IS THE BELIEF SYSTEM AND RELIGIOUS BELIEFS OF PRE-COLONIAL TAGALOGS

Pre-colonial Filipinos believed in the immortality  of the soul and in life after death   they also believed in the existence of a number  of gods whom they worship and made offerings  to according to rank  for example:  Bathalang Maykapal (Creator), Idinayale  (god of agriculture), Sidapa (god of death),  Balangaw (rainbow god), Mandarangan (war  god), Agni (fire god) Lalahon (goddess of  harvest), Siginarugan (god of hell), Diyan  Masalanta (goddess of love), etc.

They also showed respect for animals and  plants like the crocodile, crow,

tigmamanukin; some trees were not also  cut because they were thought to be  divine

• diseases were thought to be caused by  the temper of the environmental spirits

• Filipinos also venerated the dead by  keeping alive their memory by carving  idols of stone, gold or ivory called likha or  larawan; food, wine and other things were  also shared with the dead

• adored idols called anitos or diwatas to  whom they made offerings

• some anitos were considered bad; however, they made offerings to them too  in order to appease them or placate their  anger

• priestesses such as the babaylan/ baylana  or katalona acted as mediums to  communicate with these spirits.  ancient Filipinos are quite superstitious  and put much stock into auguries, and  magic charms

• they interpreted signs in nature like the  flight of birds, the barking of dogs, the  singing of lizards, and the like, as good or  bad omens depending on the  circumstances

• they also consulted with the pangatauhan,  or soothsayers, to tell their fortunes

22. Ancient beliefs and customs of the tagalogs


Answer:

Ancient Tagalogs initially believed that the first deity of the sun and moon was Bathala, as he is a primordial deity and the deity of everything. Later on, the title of deity of the moon was passed on to his favorite daughter, Mayari, while the title of deity of the sun was passed on to his grandson and honorary son, Apolaki.

29. based on your assumptions,describe the community of the tagalogs prior to the arrival of the spaniards and chistianizations of the filipinos in terms of concepts of community mores,values,and beliefs​


Answer:

The indigenous religious beliefs of the Tagalog people (sometimes referred to as Anitism, or, less accurately, using the general term "animism") were well documented by Spanish missionaries, mostly in the form of epistolary accounts (relaciones) and as entries in the various dictionaries put together by missionary friars.

Archeological and linguistic evidence indicates that these beliefs date back to the arrival of Austronesian peoples, although elements were later syncretistically adapted from Hinduism, Mahayana Buddhism, and Islam. Many of these indigenous beliefs persist to this day, in sycretistic forms discussed by scholars as Philippine variations of Folk Islam and Folk Catholicism.
