10. meaning of residual waste


Residual waste refers to the material that remains after the process of waste treatment has taken place. Such treatment can include agricultural, industrial and mining. It can also be applied in a more domestic sense, referring to the household rubbish not able to be recycled, re-used or composted.

Answer:

1. Recyclable:

Typical materials that are recycled include iron and steel scrap, aluminum cans, glass bottles, paper, wood, and plastics. The materials reused in recycling serve as substitutes for raw materials obtained from such increasingly scarce natural resources as petroleum, natural gas, coal, mineral ores, and trees.

Answer:

The residual waste is also collected from the households. As it is not practicable to material-recycle the residual waste, it is incinerated at combined heat and power plants. Note that residual waste is collected at the household properties. Hence, people do not need to walk with this waste to the recycling stations.


18. advantages and disadvantages of residual waste​


Answer:

One advantage of managing residual waste is that it allows for the recovery of valuable materials that would otherwise be lost. By sorting and processing residual waste, it is possible to recover materials such as metals, plastics, and glass that can be recycled and used again. This can help reduce the amount of waste that ends up in landfills and can also save natural resources by reducing the need to extract new raw materials.

Another advantage of managing residual waste is that it can create jobs and generate revenue. The process of sorting and processing residual waste requires labor, and the sale of recovered materials can generate income for waste management companies and governments. This can help boost the local economy and provide employment opportunities.

However, there are also several disadvantages to managing residual waste. One major disadvantage is the cost. Sorting and processing residual waste is a labor-intensive and costly process, and the recovered materials may not always have a high enough value to offset the costs. Additionally, managing residual waste requires infrastructure and equipment, such as sorting facilities and trucks for transporting waste, which can also be expensive to maintain.

Another disadvantage of managing residual waste is the potential environmental impacts. The process of sorting and processing waste can generate air and water pollution, and if not done properly, it can also lead to the release of hazardous materials into the environment. Additionally, the transportation of waste to sorting facilities and the disposal of any waste that cannot be recovered can also have negative environmental impacts.

In conclusion, while there are some advantages to managing residual waste, such as the recovery of valuable materials and the potential for job creation and revenue generation, there are also several disadvantages that should be considered, including the high cost and potential environmental impacts.
