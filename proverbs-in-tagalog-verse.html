1. explain verse proverbs 1 verse 7​


Answer:

Proverbs 1:7 NIV

"The fear of the LORD is the beginning of knowledge, but fools despise wisdom and discipline."

The "fear of the LORD" means a loving reverence of a believer for God that includes his/her submission to the Lord and to the commands of God's Word. And God is the source of all knowledge and wisdom.

"Fools" are those who "hate knowledge" (refer to Proverbs 1:22). Meaning these are the people who despise the discipline that the commands of God's Word may bring and are full of themselves and don't acknowledge the Lord as the source of wisdom and knowledge.

What does Proverbs 6:20 says about children

Answer:

Great parents collaborate to teach their children the rules of living a safe and prosperous life. Great children recall and put into practice what their parents have taught them. God bestowed various benefits on parents over children, including experience, wisdom, a long-term perspective, immense care for them, and objective awareness of their children's flaws. The first step toward success and fortune is to follow your parents' advice. Simple? Yes. Easy? No.

How much heartache and trouble might you have spared, dear reader, if you had obeyed your parents better? Your parents were far smarter than you when God brought you to them at conception and birth, no matter how ignorant they may have been (Pr 22:15).The proverb, on the other hand, talks largely about intelligent and understanding parents, who are a fantastic advantage for children if they would humbly heed and follow their advice.

Nowadays, most people laugh at children who disobey their parents. Parents are criticized for being uninformed, out of touch with reality, too selfish or conservative, unable to grasp how attitudes and behavior have evolved, solely interested in denying their children of possibilities and pleasure, and so on. As a result, it is only a rare youngster who can resist peer pressure and this terrible worldview in order to respectfully evaluate and obey his parents' regulations.

30. reflection on verse proverbs 14:16​


ANSWER:

Here is a wise man compared to a fool, showing both their attitudes and their actions. Follow closely to learn the lesson for your life. You should see both the character and the conduct of men, and you should learn and remember the differences well. Job also taught the first half of this lesson with similar words in his essay about wisdom (Job 28:28).

A wise man has fear. He has reverent fear of God, which is the beginning of wisdom and the whole duty of man (Pr 9:10; Eccl 12:13; Ps 111:10). He fears his own heart, for he knows how wicked it is (Ps 19:12-13; 51:10; 119:36; Jer 17:9). He fears authority, for God has put others over him (24:21; Eccl 8:1-5; Eph 6:1-3,5-8; I Pet 3:1-2; Heb 13:17).

But the fool has impetuous anger. He is angry against God, for not giving him all he wants and for restricting his life (Pr 19:3; Ps 2:1-3; Luke 19:14). He is angry against the righteous, for living a more holy life than his (Mark 15:10; Eccl 4:4; Acts 13:45). He is angry against authority, for he refuses to obey others (Pr 21:9; II Pet 2:10-12; Deu 21:20).

Cain was a fool; he impetuously envied Abel, because God rejected his offering (Gen 4:4-8). Uzziah was a fool; he angrily entered the temple, though the priests warned him (II Chron 26:16-21). Herodias raged against John, for reproving her adulterous marriage to Herod (Mark 6:17-28). These three arrogantly opposed God and His servants in rage.

A wise man’s fear causes him to depart from evil (Pr 3:7; 14:27; 16:6). He sees judgment coming, and he turns from his wickedness in godly repentance (Pr 16:17; 22:3). When he hears a warning from the Lord, he listens to it and obeys it (Pr 17:10; Ps 19:11; Eze 3:21).

But the fool’s impetuous anger causes him to be confident in doing evil. He believes he is right and nothing will happen to him (Pr 7:22; 14:12; 26:12). There is no glory in such confidence – it is a horrible sin based in scorn, pride, stubbornness, and rebellion.
