14. Where was the location of tropical storm Vinta on December 21 2017?​


The location of tropical storm Vinta on December 21, 2017 is the Caraga - Davao Region area.

Explanation:

Bagyong Vinta is expected to make landfall over the Caraga - Davao Region area between Thursday (December 21) evening and Friday (December 22) morning. At 4:00 am today, the center of Tropical Storm Vinta was estimated based on all available data at 510 km East of Hinatuan, Surigao del Sur (09.0 °N, 130.9 °E). Tropical cyclones that form between 5 and 30 degrees North latitude usually move westward. Sometimes the winds in the middle and upper levels of the atmosphere change and direct the cyclone to the north and northwest.

The impacts of tropical cyclones that are felt directly by the area they pass are in the form of high waves, storm surges, namely rising sea levels such as high tides that come suddenly, heavy rains and strong winds. Tropical cyclones are storms of great strength. The average radius of a tropical cyclone is 150 to 200 km. Tropical cyclones form over vast oceans which generally have a warm sea surface temperature of more than 26.5°C. Strong winds swirling near its center have wind speeds of over 63 km/h. Technically, a tropical cyclone is defined as a synoptic scale non-frontal low pressure system that grows over warm waters with areas of convective cloud and maximum wind speeds of at least 34 knots in more than half of the area surrounding its center, and lasting at least six hours.

Sometimes at the center of a tropical cyclone, an area with relatively low wind speeds and no clouds is formed, known as the eye of the cyclone. The eye diameter of a cyclone varies from 10 to 100 km. The eye of the cyclone is surrounded by an eye wall, which is a ring-shaped area that can reach a thickness of 16 km, which is the area where there are the highest wind speeds and the greatest rainfall. The average lifespan of a tropical cyclone is from 3 to 18 days. Because tropical cyclone energy is obtained from warm oceans, tropical cyclones will weaken or become extinct when they move and enter cold water areas or enter land.

The twenty-ninth and final named storm of the 2019 Pacific typhoon season, the origins of Phanfone can be traced to an upper-level low which had formed near the Caroline Islands and gradually organized into a tropical depression on December 19. Moving generally west-northwestard, the system intensified into a tropical storm on December 22 and moved into the Philippine Area of Responsibility on the following day as it continued gaining strength. Phanfone intensified to typhoon status shortly before making its first landfalls over the Eastern Visayas region on Christmas Eve. Further intensification ensued until Christmas Day despite Phanfone making several landfalls over the central Philippine islands, peaking at 150 km/h (90 mph) 10-minute sustained winds with a central pressure dropping to 970 hPa. Phanfone maintained its typhoon strength for several hours as it exited the Philippine landmass before unfavorable conditions caused it to rapidly deteriorate and dissipate over the South China Sea.

Phanfone crossed the central Philippines after the stronger Kammuri struck nearly the same region merely weeks prior, with a track fairly similar to 2013’s Haiyan.[1] The system caused destruction in the regions of Eastern Visayas, Western Visayas, and Mimaropa. The total fatalities of the said typhoon is 50 deaths (with 55 people missing, and over 300 injured) and the damages is at $67.2 million or roughly ₱3.44 billion.[2][3]
