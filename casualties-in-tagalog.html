8. what is the best method for assessing a casualty?​


Answer:

The best method for assessing a casualty in an emergency situation is to perform a quick primary survey followed by a more thorough secondary survey. A primary survey is a quick assessment of a casualty to find and correct any life-threatening issues. It often follows a structure such as DR ABC (or DRSABC).

16. Total Casualties In Kursk In WW2​


Answer:

An estimated total of 70–85 million people perished, or about 3% of the 2.3 billion (est.) people on Earth in 1940. Deaths directly caused by the war (including military and civilian fatalities) are estimated at 50–56 million, with an additional estimated 19–28 million deaths from war-related disease and famine.

The Red Army defences had held firm but a great cost of life. Although specific numbers are still debated amongst historians, it's estimated the Battle of Kursk caused around 800,000 Soviet casualties and 200,000 German casualties.

20. transporting casualty reflection​


Answer:

Transporting casualties is a critical task in emergency response and requires careful consideration of various factors such as the nature and severity of injuries, the location and accessibility of the casualty, and the available resources and equipment. Reflecting on the transport process can help identify areas for improvement and ensure that future transport operations are conducted safely and efficiently.

Some points to consider when reflecting on transporting casualties include:

Communication: Communication between responders during the transport process is crucial to ensure that everyone is aware of the situation and the status of the casualty. Reflection can help identify any breakdowns in communication and ways to improve it.

Equipment and resources: The availability and quality of equipment and resources used during the transport process can impact the safety and comfort of the casualty. Reflecting on the equipment used and considering ways to improve or upgrade it can help ensure the best possible outcomes for the casualty.

Response time: Response time is a critical factor in emergency situations. Reflecting on the time it took to transport the casualty and identifying ways to reduce it can help improve overall response times and increase the chances of a positive outcome.

Safety protocols: The safety of both the casualty and the responders must be a top priority during transport. Reflecting on the safety protocols used during the transport process can help identify areas for improvement and ensure that future transport operations are conducted safely.

Post-transport care: Once the casualty has been transported, it is important to provide appropriate post-transport care. Reflecting on the care provided and identifying ways to improve it can help ensure that the casualty receives the best possible care and support.

Overall, reflecting on the transporting of casualties can help identify areas for improvement and ensure that future transport operations are conducted safely and efficiently. It is important to continually assess and reflect on emergency response procedures to ensure that they are up-to-date and effective.

22. How should you warm a casualty with hypothermia?


Answer:

Use layers of dry blankets or coats to warm the person. Cover the person's head, leaving only the face exposed. Insulate the person's body from the cold ground. If you're outside, lay the person on his or her back on a blanket or other warm surface.

23. if there is a fire nearby casualty


Remember to GET OUT, STAY OUT and CALL 9-1-1 or your local emergency phone number.

Yell "Fire!" several times and go outside right away. If you live in a building with elevators, use the stairs. Leave all your things where they are and save yourself.

If closed doors or handles are warm or smoke blocks your primary escape route, use your second way out. Never open doors that are warm to the touch.

If you must escape through smoke, get low and go under the smoke to your exit. Close doors behind you.

If smoke, heat or flames block your exit routes, stay in the room with doors closed. Place a wet towel under the door and call the fire department or 9-1-1. Open a window and wave a brightly colored cloth or flashlight to signal for help.

Once you are outside, go to your meeting place and then send one person to call the fire department. If you cannot get to your meeting place, follow your family emergency communication plan.
