8. what must happen to a body of rock before exfoliation can occur?​


For rocks to reach the condition for the exfoliation process to occur, rocks must first be in a place where there is a heat source for them to heat up that will then cause them (1) to expand. Then it must cool down for it (2) to contract. This will cause (3) joints to form in the outer part of the rock. Then lastly, the (4) outer part will break.

The process I described above is called exfoliation. Exfoliation is defined as the phenomenon wherein “certain massive rocks tend to scale off along joints, especially at corners and edges, and thereby develop bowlder-like forms, which are often inclosed in concentric shells.” (Blackwelder, 1925)

Exfoliation is caused by Weathering. In earth science, weathering is defined as the process that causes rocks on the surface of the Earth to disintegrate. There are two kinds of weathering: Mechanical Weathering and Chemical Weathering.

Exfoliation is a kind of Mechanical Weathering because, unlike Chemical Weathering, the composition of the rock is still retained.

Rock exfoliation resulted in magnetic sights found all over the world like:

A. The exfoliation domes in Yosemite National Park

B. The Quiet City of Rocks found in Idaho

Other examples of mechanical weathering include frost action, abrasion caused by water and/or wind, salt wedging, impacts, and collisions.

As for chemical weathering, examples include rock deformation caused by acid rain, fungi, and lichens, oxidation, and hydration. Rocks, where you see the visible presence of rocks, are a kind of chemical weathering caused by the process of oxidation.

The earth is so much older than us and the rocks and earth that you see, have changed so much from their original form. These things are important to study so we can marvel at how even, something so dull and seemingly steady form of rock has transformed multiple times to get to its present state.
