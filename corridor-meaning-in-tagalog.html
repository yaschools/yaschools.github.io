14. PLZ HELP The rooms along one side of a hotel corridor are numbered 101, 102, 103, ... , from east to west. Continuing with consecutive integers, the rooms along the other side of corridor are numbered west to east. There are the same number of rooms along each side of the corridor. If room 111 is opposite room 128 how many rooms are along the corridor?


Answer:

Step-by-step explanation:

Let's assume that there are 'n' rooms along each side of the corridor.

We know that the room opposite to 111 is 128. Since the rooms are numbered consecutively, the difference between their numbers is the same as the number of rooms between them. Therefore, the distance between room 111 and room 128 is 128 - 111 - 1 = 16.

Since there are 'n' rooms on each side of the corridor, there are a total of 2n rooms. We can divide these rooms into two groups of n rooms each, one group on each side of the corridor.

The distance between room 111 and the end of its group of rooms (i.e., room 101) is 111 - 101 = 10. Similarly, the distance between room 128 and the end of its group of rooms (i.e., room 2n) is 2n - 128 = n - 128 + 111 = n - 17.

We know that the distance between room 111 and room 128 is 16, and this distance can be expressed as the sum of the distances between room 111 and the end of its group of rooms, and between room 128 and the end of its group of rooms:

16 = 10 + (n - 17)

n = 23

Therefore, there are 23 rooms on each side of the corridor, and a total of 46 rooms along the corridor.

23. explain what is philippine electrical code (pec) Article 1.4.3-Stairways, ramps, corridors, and other means of egress shall be provided with emergency lighting.​


Answer:

Article 1.4.3 of the Philippine Electrical Code (PEC) states that stairways, ramps, corridors, and other means of egress must be provided with emergency lighting. This is an important safety requirement that helps to ensure that people can safely exit a building in the event of a power outage or other emergency that causes a loss of normal lighting.

Emergency lighting is a backup lighting system that automatically turns on during a power outage or other emergency, providing enough illumination to allow people to safely exit a building. Emergency lighting must meet certain standards, including the duration of the backup power supply and the brightness of the lighting, as specified in the PEC and other relevant regulations.

Stairways, ramps, corridors, and other means of egress are areas where people are likely to be moving during an emergency, so it is especially important that these areas be well-lit. Properly installed and maintained emergency lighting can help to prevent panic and confusion during an emergency, and can significantly reduce the risk of injury or death due to falls, collisions, or other hazards.

Overall, Article 1.4.3 of the PEC emphasizes the importance of emergency lighting as an essential safety feature in buildings, and provides guidelines for ensuring that emergency lighting is installed and maintained properly in stairways, ramps, corridors, and other means of egress.

30. place of possible hazard in the classroom and corridors


Answer:

Entrance and exitsWindowsFloorsWalls

Explanation:

Entrances and Exits

Steps and entrance ramps should have sturdy handrails with two levels for both child and adult use. Doors should remain unlocked (or have panic bars) so they provide easy access to and from the building. Hallways should be clearly lighted and remain uncluttered in case of emergency exit.

Windows

Children love to look out windows, but they can fall through open windows or screens or break through glass panes. You can create a barrier in front of low windows by securing plastic safety gate panels to the walls to form a "fence" in front of the window. Lock windows or attach window guards that limit the distance a window may open. To prevent head entrapment, windows should not open more than 3 1/2".

Window coverings can also present safety hazards. Be sure to remove or securely tie any cords which could cause strangulation.

Floors

A young child can spot anything on the floor, and whatever it is, it will probably go in the mouth! Therefore, floors and floor coverings should be clean and free of debris or tripping hazards. Use carpet tape or rubber backing to prevent rugs from curling or slipping and carefully check for pins, staples, or other items which can hide in the carpet pile. Check floor tiles and baseboards for loose material, protruding nails, or splinters. Inspect carefully in corners and under furniture for paperclips, toy parts, and other small items.

Walls

Inspection of walls should include electrical outlets, wall coverings, bulletin boards, and pictures or decoration. First, all electrical outlets should be securely covered when not in use. While small "push-in" covers may meet licensing standards, they can become a choking hazard if a curious child removes them. Outlets that will not be used can be covered with solid closed electrical plates, available at hardware stores. If an outlet is used regularly, you can install a hinged or screw-in outlet guard that also covers the electrical cord end.
