PHENOMENONThey are all around us, yet some are easier to see than others. Phenomena are described as "observable occurrences that occur in a natural or planned system." Lightning, earthquakes, tsunamis, volcanic eruptions, tornadoes, and similar events are typical examples of natural occurrences.

HEIDEGGER'S TEACHINGSHeidegger described the universe in terms of two contexts: the common world and the subjective world. He consequently portrayed the interconnectedness of these clearly delineated realms as the phenomena of worldhood. As a result of their interactions with entities in the world, Heidegger created the concept of Dasein.Heidegger's philosophy is sometimes used erratically in research, and there is considerable discussion over its impact on how interpretative phenomenological research is conducted.Martin Heidegger, a German philosopher, said that a phenomena is that which becomes evident for us. He was arguing that phenomena are created as a result of how we interact with it. The majority of phenomenologists do not think that people create phenomenological experiences.SYNTAXPhenomenology is Heidegger's preferred approach of ontology. Being is to be comprehended by means of the phenomenological method, he continues, and is the route of entry to what is to become the topic of ontology.
