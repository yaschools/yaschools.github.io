Answer:

The Limit of the Rational Function Theorem and Limit of a Radical of a Function are both concepts in calculus that deal with determining the behavior of a function as its input approaches a particular value.

The Limit of a Rational Function Theorem states that if a function can be expressed as a ratio of two polynomials, then the limit of the function as the input approaches a particular value can be found by evaluating the limit of the ratio of the highest degree terms of the numerator and denominator. Specifically, if the degree of the numerator is less than the degree of the denominator, then the limit of the function as the input approaches infinity or negative infinity is zero. If the degree of the numerator is equal to the degree of the denominator, then the limit is equal to the ratio of the coefficients of the highest degree terms. If the degree of the numerator is greater than the degree of the denominator, then the limit does not exist.

The Limit of a Radical of a Function refers to the behavior of a function that contains a radical (square root, cube root, etc.) as its input approaches a particular value. To evaluate the limit of such a function, you can use algebraic manipulation to simplify the function and eliminate the radical, and then apply the Limit of a Rational Function Theorem to find the limit. Alternatively, you can use special techniques such as conjugate multiplication or L'Hopital's Rule to evaluate the limit directly without simplifying the function.
