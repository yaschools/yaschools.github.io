23. Explain the meaning of the designations D and L as used to specify the configuration of a monosaccharide​


Answer:

The designations D and L refer to the relationship between the two optical isomers of a monosaccharide. D-monosaccharides are those with the highest rotation when observed in an optical activity experiment, while L-monosaccharides have the opposite rotation. The terms D and L refer to the Latin words dexter (right) and laevus (left), respectively.

The designations D and L are used to describe the stereochemistry or three-dimensional structure of monosaccharides, which are simple sugars. These sugars have a central carbon atom, which is typically called the anomeric carbon, that is connected to a hydroxyl (-OH) group, a hydrogen atom (-H), and a side chain. The orientation of the hydroxyl group on the anomeric carbon determines the configuration of the sugar, which can either be D or L.
