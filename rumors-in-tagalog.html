Which refers to the spreading of information, ideas, or rumor​s?

The transmission of information—facts, arguments, rumors, half-truths, or lies—in order to affect public opinion is known as propaganda.

Propaganda is communication aimed primarily at influencing an audience and furthering a goal. It may or may not be objective, and it may offer data selectively to encourage a specific synthesis.

Propaganda's purpose. Rather than just communicating information, the goal of propaganda is to actively influence people's ideas or behaviors.

During World War I, when posters and films were used against opponents to motivate army enrollment and attract public opinion, the term "propaganda" became widely used in the United States. Propaganda evolved into a modern political technique for generating positive sentiment among a diverse demography and winning public support.

Propaganda is a style of message presentation that is explicitly focused at influencing people's attitudes or behavior rather than presenting objective information.

23. the relationship between suspicion and rumors (explain)


Answer:

Suspicion is the act of suspecting something or someone, especially of something wrong while rumor is (countable) a statement or claim of questionable accuracy, from no known reliable source, usually spread by word of mouth.


24. Why is rumor-mongering a bad practice?


A "rumor" is a doubtful truth, which means there is no proof. Circulating such things causes various opinions about the subject of the rumor. You are stepping at the right of the subject to explain himself and deny the accusation (if it's not really true). People get the wrong impression of the person as a result. You already judged him without knowing the truth first. You do not only affect the life of the accused person but as well as yours, you will have a reputation of circulating rumors uncontrollably. Thus, rumor-mongering is a bad practice.

29. Do you know where the rumors originated


Answer:

rumor (n.)

late 14c., from Old French rumor "commotion, widespread noise or report" (Modern French rumeur), from Latin rumorem (nominative rumor) "noise, clamor, common talk, hearsay, popular opinion," related to ravus "hoarse," from PIE *reu- "to bellow." Related: Rumorous.
