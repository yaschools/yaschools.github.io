6. Subject: LAW What is… 1) Principle of legality 2) Principle of separation of powers 3) Principle of individual freedom 4) Representative principle


1.The principle of legality, also known as the rule of law, is a fundamental legal principle that states that all laws must be applied and enforced in a fair and consistent manner. It requires that all laws must be written down and made publicly available, and that they must be applied equally to all people regardless of their social or economic status. This principle is based on the idea that no one should be above the law, and that everyone should have access to justice. The principle of legality also requires that any punishment for breaking the law must be proportionate to the crime committed. This means that punishments should not be excessive or arbitrary. The principle of legality is an important part of many legal systems around the world, as it helps to ensure fairness and justice for all citizens.

2.The Principle of Separation of Powers is a fundamental principle of constitutional government. It is based on the idea that the powers of government should be divided among different branches, each with its own set of responsibilities and authority. This separation of powers helps to ensure that no one branch becomes too powerful and can prevent any one branch from abusing its power.

The three branches of government in the United States are the legislative, executive, and judicial branches. The legislative branch is responsible for making laws, the executive branch is responsible for enforcing laws, and the judicial branch is responsible for interpreting laws. Each branch has its own set of powers and responsibilities that are outlined in the Constitution. The separation of powers helps to ensure that each branch can act independently and without interference from other branches. This helps to protect individual rights by ensuring that no one branch can become too powerful or abuse its power.

3. The Principle of Individual Freedom is the belief that each individual has the right to live their life as they choose, free from interference or control by others. This includes the right to think and express oneself freely, to pursue one's own interests and goals, and to make decisions about one's own life without fear of retribution or coercion. It also includes the right to privacy, freedom of association, and freedom from discrimination. This principle is fundamental to many democratic societies, which recognize that individuals should be able to make their own choices without interference from government or other external forces.

4. The Representative Principle is a fundamental concept in democratic theory that states that the people should have the right to choose their representatives in government. This principle is based on the idea that citizens should be able to select those who will make decisions on their behalf and be held accountable for their actions. It is also based on the belief that all citizens should have an equal say in the decisions made by their government. This principle is often seen as a cornerstone of democracy, as it allows citizens to have a direct say in how their government operates and ensures that all voices are heard.

LEGAL SEPARATION

A legal separation does not officially end a marriage. The parties are still legally married and cannot remarry or enter into domestic partnerships with others. Legal separation is a reasonable choice for couples who aren’t ready to get a divorce but who want to live separately and decide on issues such as custody and asset division. Some couples prefer legal separation to divorce if potential reconciliation could be in the future. Others avoid divorce for religious reasons or to retain benefits such as health insurance. During legal separation, you can ask a judge to decide on all the same issues as in a divorce, but you will still be legally married.

ANNULMENT

A legal annulment does not end a valid marriage, but rather declares that the marriage never existed in the first place. An annulment may be appropriate if something made the marriage invalid or never officially legal. For example, if one spouse was already married at the time of the second alleged marriage. The Pennsylvania courts may declare a marriage void and grant an annulment request if the spouses are under the age of 16 and lack parental consent, either party was under the influence at the time of marriage, either party was incurably impotent, or if either party entered into the marriage due to coercion or fraud.
