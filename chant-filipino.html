Understanding ritual dance

Ritual dance is a form of dance that is related to rituals, namely procedures for religious ceremonies. Belief in supernatural/supernatural powers is often manifested in various ways, such as using offerings, saying prayers, presenting sacred songs and dancing.

The Function of Dance as a Ritual Ceremony

The function of dance as a means of ritual ceremonies, took place at a time when human civilization was still very backward. Art life at that time did not recognize the existence of musical instruments, clothing, and movements such as art today.

Usually the dance for this ritual ceremony involves individuals, entire families, entire villages, even involving the State, and is carried out at the start of an event. For example, events of birth, death, farming, hunting, asking for rain, going to war, and so on. A ceremony is an act or series of actions carried out according to customs or religion that marks the sacredness of an event.

These actions are usually related to everyday life, the environment, nature, and rulers. The art of dance for ritual purposes must have predetermined rules.

There are several rules in the implementation of ritual dance, namely:

a. The dance must be held at a chosen place, usually a place that is considered sacred.

b. It must be held at the chosen time, according to the intent and purpose of the ritual.

c. Danced by selected dancers, generally those who are considered holy or who are in a state of "not dirty".

d. Usually requires a set of offerings. The offerings can be simple and can also be very many in number and kinds.

e. There are no spectators, because those who attend the ceremony are considered as participants of the ceremony or congregation.

25. define the following1.Mozarabic Chant2. Byzantine Chant3. Gallican Chant4. Ambrosian Chant​


ANSWER:

This is the meaning of Mozarabic Chant, Byzantine Chant, Gallican Chant, Ambrosian Chant

1. Mozarabic chant, also called Visigothic chant or ancient Spanish chant, Latin liturgical chant of the Christian church on the Iberian Peninsula from its beginnings about the 5th century until its suppression at the end of the 11th century in favour of the liturgy and Gregorian chant of the Roman Catholic Church.

2. Byzantine chant, monophonic, or unison, liturgical chant of the Greek Orthodox church during the Byzantine Empire (330–1453) and down to the 16th century; in modern Greece the term refers to ecclesiastical music of any period. ... The music is unrelated to that of ancient Greece and Byzantium.

3. Gallican chant refers to the liturgical plainchant repertory of the Gallican rite of the Roman Catholic Church in Gaul, prior to the introduction and development of elements of the Roman rite from which Gregorian chant evolved.

4.Ambrosian chant, monophonic, or unison, chant that accompanies the Latin mass and canonical hours of the Ambrosian rite. The word Ambrosian is derived from St. Ambrose, bishop of Milan (374–397), from which comes the occasional designation of this rite as Milanese.
