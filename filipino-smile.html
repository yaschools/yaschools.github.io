10. Directions: List down 20 types/examples of non-verbal communication commonly used by Filipinos. List down the meaning on the second column.NON-VERBAL COMMUNICATION MEANING OR CONTEXTExample: Smiling A smile generally indicates happiness or contentmentSUBJECT : ORAL COMMUNICATION​


1) One's index finger

The Filipino hand gesture of "Lagot ka," which is akin to the expressions "You're in trouble" or "You're dead meat," involves wagging and pointing the index finger.

2) Pointing the lip

This gesture has a good chance of being misinterpreted as a kiss, but it's not at all that. Filipinos eventually developed the habit of pointing with their lips rather than their fingers.

3) Mouth Open

This Filipino hand gesture actually indicates that the recipient didn't quite hear or understand what was said.

4) Bill, Please

The next time you eat out with friends and are about to pay the check, use this Filipino hand gesture.

5) Attractive

The hand gesture depicted above is the non-verbal Filipino expression for "handsome" or "good-looking."

6) Peace sign

The well-known peace sign. You're probably already familiar with this "peace" hand gesture, which is a V formed by the index and middle fingers.

7) Money

Although some Filipinos use this gesture to indicate "okay," it is more common for them to indicate "money" or one of the slang terms for "money" (pera, kwarta, datung, anda).

8) Good

Another common Filipino hand move is the thumbs-up. The thumbs-up sign is largely used globally in the same way.

9) Mano Po

Pagmamano or "mano po" is a distinctive sign of respect used by Filipinos. You place the back of an older or elderly relative's hand—such as an uncle or an aunt—on your forehead.

10. The "I Don't Know" Phrase

The expression has a different meaning. This facial expression, along with a quick bow and a furrowed brow, conveys "I don't know."

11) Nodding and grinning while raising eyebrows

This is one informal and speedy greeting used by Filipinos to acknowledge someone they have seen.

12) Lowering the head while holding out the arms

These gestures are frequently used when walking past two people having a conversation or when crossing a room where someone is watching TV. This action, when followed by a "excuse me," is regarded as polite and respectful.

13) Using the hands to draw a square or rectangle in the air

When asking for the menu or the bill in restaurants, Filipinos frequently do this.

14) Quiet gazes

Filipino parents typically tell their kids to stop whatever it is they're doing when they give them the fiery-eyes-silent look.

15) Eye contact

Is the person looking you in the eye? If so, is the intensity too high or just right?

16) Expression of the face

What is visible on their face? Is it emotionless and mask-like, or is it interesting and emotionally present?

17) Voice inflection

Does the person's voice convey friendliness, assurance, and interest, or does it sound strained and blocked?

18) Pose and gesture

Is their body at ease or rigid and motionless? Do they have relaxed or tense shoulders?

19) Touch

Is there any physical contact? Is it suitable for the circumstances? Do you find it uncomfortable?

20)  Intensity

Does the person seem over-the-top and dramatic, or flat, cool, and uninterested?
