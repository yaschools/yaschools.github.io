5. what are mass noun,count noun,possessive noun and collective noun​


Answer:

Mass nouns are nouns that, by their very nature, are plural. These are also called uncountable nouns or noncount nouns. Examples: She went to get glasses of water for everyone.

In linguistics, a count noun (also countable noun) is a noun that can be modified by a numeral and that occurs in both singular and plural forms, and that can co-occur with quantificational determiners like every, each, several, etc.

A possessive noun is a noun that shows possession or belonging. This will generally include an's for a singular person possessing one or more persons, places, or things, called a singular possessive noun; and an s' for more than one person possessing singular or plural persons, places, or things.

A collective noun is a word or phrase that refers to a group of people or things as one entity. ... Collective nouns represent more than one person or thing in a class.

17. What is Possessive Nouns? What is Collective Nouns?


Answer:

A possessive nouns are types of nouns that show ownership or connection. In most cases, a possessive noun is formed by adding an apostrophe +s to the noun, or if the noun is plural and already ends in s, only an apostrophe needs to be added.

Collective nouns are names for a collection or a number of people or things. Words like group, herd, and array are collective noun examples.

21. are countries are mass noun count noun or possessive noun​


Answer:

Countable nouns (or count nouns) are those that refer to something that can be counted. They have both singular and plural forms (e.g. cat/cats; woman/women; country/countries). In the singular, they can be preceded by a or an. Most nouns come into this category.

QUESTION?

are countries are mass noun count noun or possessive noun

ANSWER!

Count Nouns name everything that can be counted (2 bananas, 4 classes, 1 country, 5 religions, 100 computers). Mass Nouns name everything that cannot be counted, things such as liquids (Water, oil) abstract ideas (knowledge, advice) and things too small to be counted (rice, sand)
