15. Consider the queuing delay in a router buffer, where the packet experiences a delay as it waits to be transmitted onto the link. the length of the queuing delay of a specific packet will depend on the number of earlier-arriving packets that are queued and waiting for transmission onto the link. if the queue is empty and no other packet is currently being transmitted, then our packet’s queuing delay will be zero. on the other hand, if the traffic is heavy and many other packets are also waiting to be transmitted, the queuing delay will be long.


Answer:

yes, that is correct. The length of the queuing delay of a specific packet in a router buffer will depend on the number of earlier-arriving packets that are queued and waiting for transmission onto the link. If the queue is empty and no other packet is currently being transmitted, then the packet's queuing delay will be zero. On the other hand, if the traffic is heavy and many other packets are also waiting to be transmitted, the queuing delay will be longer.

This delay is also known as the "Queueing delay" which is the amount of time that a packet spends waiting in the buffer before it can be transmitted. This delay is caused by network congestion, where the router's buffer is full and packets have to wait for other packets to be transmitted before they can be sent. The queueing delay is an important metric in network performance and can be used to determine the quality of service (QoS) provided by the network.

19. Distinguish the 3 kinds of delay​


Answer:

There are three main types of delay:

1. Processing delay: It refers to the time taken by a system to process a given input and produce an output. This type of delay occurs in digital systems such as computers and communication systems. Processing delay can be caused by a variety of factors, including the complexity of the task, the speed of the system, and the amount of data to be processed.

2. Transmission delay: It refers to the time taken by a system to transmit data from one point to another. This type of delay is caused by the limitations of the transmission medium, such as the bandwidth of the channel and the distance between the sender and receiver. The greater the distance and the lower the bandwidth, the greater the transmission delay.

3. Propagation delay: It refers to the time taken by a signal to travel from one point to another. This type of delay is caused by the physical characteristics of the transmission medium, such as the speed of light or electricity. Propagation delay is also affected by the distance between the sender and receiver and can be significant over long distances.
