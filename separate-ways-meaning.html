Substances and Mixtures

Substances contain one type of atom or molecule while mixtures contain more than one atom or molecule that isn't chemically bonded. In the following statements, we will determine their definition as well as other information such as their types.

AnswerPureChemically, moleculesHomogenousPhysical, reactionCompoundCombinedComponents

Additional Explanation

Elements are the basic form of matter, composed of only ONE type of atom. For example, an H element has a hydrogen atom. It cannot be broken or decomposed into a simpler form. A compound, on the other side, is composed of two or more different elements in a fixed ratio and bound together through chemical means. Examples of compounds are water ([tex]H_2O[/tex]) and table sugar ([tex]C_{12}H_{22}C_{11}[/tex]).

When two or more atoms are combined, we call them molecules. As a general idea, "All compounds are molecules, but not all molecules are called compounds." Take note, the combined elements must be of different types of atoms for a molecule to be a compound.

We know that matters (i.e., anything that has space and mass) are classified into two groups - pure substances and mixtures. Pure substances are matters that have a distinct composition, wherein they cannot be decomposed into smaller forms through physical means. This can only be done when the substance undergoes a chemical reaction.

On the contrary, mixtures are composed of two or more pure substances and can be broken into their components. They can be homogenous (i.e., uniform throughout the mixture) or heterogeneous (i.e., the components can be distinguished using the naked eye).

Example of pure substances:

NaCl (salt)[tex]\rm{H_2O}[/tex] (water)

Example of mixtures:
