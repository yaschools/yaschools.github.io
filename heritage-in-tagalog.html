8. The world heritage? ​


The world heritage-Machu Picchu-Great Wall of China-Rice Terraces of the Philippine Cordilleras-Yellowstone National Park

Machu Picchu

-Machu Picchu is an Incan citadel set high in the Andes Mountains in Peru, above the Urubamba River valley. Built in the 15th century and later abandoned, it’s renowned for its sophisticated dry-stone walls that fuse huge blocks without the use of mortar, intriguing buildings that play on astronomical alignments and panoramic views. Its exact former use remains a mystery.

Great Wall of China

-The Great Wall of China is a series of fortifications that were built across the historical northern borders of ancient Chinese states and Imperial China as protection against various nomadic groups from the Eurasian Steppe.

Rice Terraces of the Philippine Cordilleras

-The Rice Terraces of the Philippine Cordilleras are a World Heritage Site consisting of a complex of rice terraces on the island of Luzon in the Philippines.

Yellowstone National Park

-Yellowstone National Park is a nearly 3,500-sq.-mile wilderness recreation area atop a volcanic hot spot. Mostly in Wyoming, the park spreads into parts of Montana and Idaho too. Yellowstone features dramatic canyons, alpine rivers, lush forests, hot springs and gushing geysers, including its most famous, Old Faithful. It's also home to hundreds of animal species, including bears, wolves, bison, elk and antelope.

16. explain the line "our heritage of greatness, for the national heritage is - lets face it - a heritage of smalness.​


Answer:

The line "our heritage of greatness, for the national heritage is - let's face it - a heritage of smallness" suggests that the speaker believes that the common understanding of national heritage is limited and does not adequately capture the greatness of the country or its people. Instead, the speaker believes that true greatness lies in something beyond what is traditionally associated with national heritage.

By using the phrase "heritage of smallness," the speaker implies that the traditional understanding of national heritage is focused on minor accomplishments or details that are not truly indicative of the country's true potential or achievements. The phrase "let's face it" is used to acknowledge that this idea may be controversial or uncomfortable, but the speaker believes it is important to recognize in order to move towards a more accurate understanding of the country's history and potential.

Overall, the line suggests that the speaker believes that there is more to be celebrated and recognized about the country than what is commonly acknowledged through its national heritage.

30. choose one speafic Tangible cultural heritage and oneintangible cultural heritage​


Answer:

Tangible Cultural Heritage’ refers to physical artefacts produced, maintained and transmitted intergenerationally in a society. It includes artistic creations, built heritage such as buildings and monuments, and other physical or tangible products of human creativity that are invested with cultural significance in a society. ‘Intangible Cultural Heritage’ indicates ‘the practices, representations, expressions, knowledge, skills – as well as the instruments, objects, artefacts and cultural spaces associated therewith – that communities, groups and, in some cases, individuals recognize as part of their Cultural Heritage’ (UNESCO, 2003). Examples of intangible heritage are oral traditions, performing arts, local knowledge, and traditional skills.

Tangible and intangible heritage require different approaches for preservation and safeguarding, which has been one of the main motivations driving the conception and ratification of the 2003 UNESCO Convention for the Safeguarding of the Intangible Cultural Heritage. The Convention stipulates the interdependence between intangible Cultural Heritage, and tangible cultural and natural heritage, and acknowledges the role of intangible Cultural Heritage as a source of cultural diversity and a driver of sustainable development. Recognizing the value of people for the expression and transmission of intangible Cultural Heritage, UNESCO spearheaded the recognition and promotion of living human treasures, ‘persons who possess to a very high degree the knowledge and skills required for performing or recreating specific elements of the intangible Cultural Heritage’.
