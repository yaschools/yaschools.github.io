22. I am neither an ocean or a sea.I am a small body of water surrounded by land. Though a river I could never be.


The Answer is Lake.

A lake is a body of water surrounded by land and located in a basin area. But not all lakes are the same. Some are formed by earthquakes, some are formed due to erosion, and some are built by humans. There are several types of lakes based on the process of their formation.

Lake Tectonic

Tectonic lakes are lakes that are formed due to the process of changing the shape of the earth's crust. Earth's crust can fold, break, and move. When an earthquake, for example, the skin of the earth can be broken. As a result, the ground surface sinks and becomes concave. The basin is filled with water and a lake is formed.

Volcanic Lake

A volcanic lake or crater lake is a lake formed as a result of volcanic activity. When a volcano erupts, the rock that covers the crater will be thrown away and leave a hole. When it rains, the crater hole will fill with water and form a lake.

Tectovulcanic lake

Tectovulcanic lakes are formed due to a combination of tectonic and volcanic processes. When a volcano erupts, some of the soil and rock covering the mountain slide. The avalanche formed a basin.

Karst Lake

Karst lake or doline is a lake formed from the erosion process or the dissolution of limestone by rainwater in calcareous rock areas. The dissolution of the rock produces a basin formation. This basin filled with rainwater and formed a lake called a doline. Karst lakes that are larger than dolines are known as uvala. The karst lake which is larger than the uvala is named Polje.

Glacial Lake

Glacial lakes are formed as a result of the dredging of the valley floor by glaciers. In summer or autumn, melting glaciers fill the hollows they pass through to form lakes. Some of the glacial lakes in the world are Great Lake, Finger Lake, and Superior Lake.

Horseshoe Lake

Horseshoe lakes are formed from eroded material that is deposited when the speed of river flow decreases. This deposition closes the river flow on the meander so that the meander is separated from the new river flow. Meander rivers that separate and fill with water then form a horseshoe-like lake. This lake is often also referred to as dead time or oxbow lake.

Dam

A dam is a lake formed by damming a river. In addition to human formation, reservoirs or dams can occur due to natural processes such as landslides.
