4. explain the nature of earthquake aftershock​


Answer:

An aftershock results from the sudden change in stress occurring within and between rocks and the previous release of stress brought on by the principal earthquake. Aftershocks occur in rocks located near the epicentre or along the fault that harboured the principal quake.

What is the aftershock of an earthquake?​

An aftershock is a minor earthquake that occurs after a bigger earthquake in the same location as the main shock, when the displaced crust adjusts to the main shock's consequences. Hundreds to thousands of instrumentally detectable aftershocks can result from large earthquakes, which gradually diminish in magnitude and frequency according to recognized rules. Some earthquakes have numerous main shocks because the major rupture occurs in two or more phases. These are referred to as doublet earthquakes, and they differ from aftershocks in that they have comparable magnitudes and virtually identical seismic waveforms.

The majority of aftershocks occur throughout the whole region of fault rupture, either along the fault plane itself or along secondary faults within the volume impacted by the initial shock's strain. Aftershocks are often detected up to a distance equal to the rupture length from the fault plane.

23. Different types of earthquake and aftershock


An aftershock is a smaller earthquake that occurs after a previous large earthquake, in the same area of the main shock. If an aftershock is larger than the main shock, the aftershock is redesignated as the main shock and the original main shock is redesignated as a foreshock. Aftershocks are formed as the crust around the displaced fault plane adjusts to the effects of the main shock.
