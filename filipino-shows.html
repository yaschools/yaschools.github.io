8. How did the filipinos show their beliefs?​


Answer:

The nature of religion in the pre-colonial Philippines is often unclear. Religions present include animism, indigenous religious beliefs and mythologies such as Anito and influences from Hinduism and Buddhism. The earliest pieces of evidence that exist are archaeological finds including Hindu–Buddhist gold statues. The earliest written evidence comes from the Laguna Copperplate Inscription, dated to around 900 CE, which uses the Buddhist–Hindu lunar calendar. With the arrival of Islam in the 14th century, the older religions gradually disappeared, and after the arrival of Ferdinand Magellan in 1521 Christianity, specifically Roman Catholicism, became the dominant religion. However, some of the indigenous peoples of the Philippines continue to practice animism today, and many of the traditions in Anito have survived in the form of Folk Catholicism.

Answer:

For many Filipinos, the time of ‘fiesta’ is an important event within the community. During the Catholic event of fiesta, the local community comes together to celebrate the special day of the patron saint of a town or ‘barangay’ (village). It is a time for feasting, bonding and paying homage to the patron saint. Houses are open to guests and plenty of food is served. The fiesta nearly always includes a Mass, but its primary purpose is a social gathering of the community. On a day-to-day level, Catholic iconography is evident throughout the Philippines. Indeed, it is common to find churches and statues of various saints all throughout the country. Moreover, many towns and cities are named after saints (for example, San Miguel [‘Saint Michael’] located in Luzon and Santa Catalina [‘Saint Catherine’] located in Visayas).

21. this shows that filipinos are hospitable people​


Answer:

Hospitality. This is the most common terminology that describes how Filipinos welcome foreigners or tourists who visit the country. This trait of the Filipinos towards their visitors is undeniably exceptional. Whenever a visitor stays in a Filipino home, there will be a feast or a grand celebration as it is somehow a tradition for us to prepare bountiful Filipino cuisine for guests to have a taste of our culture through food. In addition, a cheerful family would typically be ready to accommodate and spend time with the visitors. To add to that, Filipino families are often more than willing to share not only their meals but their entire home as well. As a matter of fact, Filipinos would usually greet their visitors with the phrase “Feel at home!” to ensure that they are at ease during their stay. For Filipinos, it is a pleasure and the country’s honor to accept foreigners as visitors and build genuine relationships and friendship with them.

26. 5 advertisments that shows filipino values​


FIVE ADVERTISEMENTS THAT SHOWS FILIPINO VALUES

Many famous products in the Philippines have become the talk of the town as a result of their commercials, which have a storyline fit for a movie. A Jollibee commercial with the tagline Kwentong Jollibee.

1. MCDONALD'S "KAREN PO"

The ad features Karen with her forgetful Grandpa, Grandpa Rudy. Her grandfather always mistaken her as the other granddaughter as Gina. In the end, Grandpa Rudy acknowledges Karen by giving a half of his burger. Then followed by saying: "Ang paborito kong apo, si Karen." My favorite granddaughter, Karen.

VALUE SHOWN: Filipinos hold families valuable especially towards the aging generation. In here, Karen is being a good granddaughter.

2. RC COLA'S AMPON

The surrealistic RC Cola's ad features about a boy being different and asked his parents if he is adopted. The whole ad  rolls differently into saying that they might be different, they are still family.

VALUE SHOWN: Adoption is a very sensitive topic in the Philippines since they value blood. The ad shows how families are formed even without the bond of blood.

3. TANG: WE ARE HOME MADE

The ad talks about the judgmental comments children and parents often faced but never understanding what is behind their stories.

VALUES SHOWN: Everyone has battles and they should be kind to each other.

4. JOLLIBEE: FOUND THE PEARL

The ad takes the audience into a conquest of finding a single pair of a missing earring then go to celebrate to the famous Fast food chain.

VALUES SHOWN: Filipinos celebrate small victories and moments. We love to celebrate as Filipinos.

5. FITA: WISH KO, SPORTS CAR

The hilarious and classic ad is about a man dividing a cracker to a homeless lady. Because of this, the elderly turned into a wish-granting fairy and the man wished for a sports car. However, his wish was  as half as the half Fita he gave.

VALUES SHOWN: Altruism is shown in the ad. In equivalent, we have bayanihan which helps the community.
