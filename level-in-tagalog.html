Emilio Aguinaldo donated his house, which is now known as the Aguinaldo Shrine, to the Philippine government to serve as a symbol of his patriotism and his contribution to the country's struggle for independence. The topmost level of Aguinaldo's house served as a watchtower that allowed the Philippine revolutionary forces to monitor the movements of the Spanish troops in the surrounding areas. From this vantage point, the revolutionaries could detect any imminent threat and prepare their defenses accordingly. This helped them to mount a successful defense against the Spanish attack during the Battle of Alapan on May 28, 1898, which ultimately led to the hoisting of the Philippine flag.

Answer:

Labor

Let First-Level Supervisors Do Their Job

by W. Earl Sasser, Jr. and Frank S. Leonard

From the Magazine (March 1980)

Performing well as a first-level supervisor is like walking the circus high wire. In both positions, the ability to maintain one’s balance when shifting forces pull in opposite directions is a measure of one’s success. First-level supervisors must be able to harmonize the demands of management, the demands of the collective work force (often represented by unions), and the demands of workers with the requirements for doing the tasks at hand. These needs are more often than not conflicting and even at times mutually exclusive. First-level supervisors usually have mixed emotions about their situation and often lose their sense of identity as they try to perform this precarious balancing act.
